# Nick's stakeholder email style (multi-topic, known recipient)

**Rule.** When Nick is writing an email to a person he knows that covers multiple things at once, the form he reaches for is a tight **numbered list of 2-4 items**, with conversational connector phrases instead of bold section headers, strategic-logic stripped where the recipient already lives in the same world, and timing folded into the relevant ask instead of standing as its own section. Match this shape any time the task is "draft an email to <named person> about <three or four things>".

**Why.** Set 2026-06-09 after he rewrote v5 of the SLCO email to Nick Giblin into the version he actually sent. v5 was structurally a memo — four prose paragraphs with topic-introducing openers ("On the SME side", "On timing"). Nick's final version was structurally a chat — numbered points, "In terms of getting input on these", timing folded into the SME paragraph as a one-line nudge, the Patrol Map dropped from item 2 to item 3 because it didn't need an action from the recipient. The general WRITINGSTYLE.md captures his voice across registers; this memory captures the specific *form* he uses for known-recipient stakeholder emails, which is a sub-genre WRITINGSTYLE doesn't cover.

**How to apply.**

*Structure:*
1. Warm, low-pressure opener (already in WRITINGSTYLE — "Hey", "Hope you're keeping well", channel acknowledgement without justification).
2. One-line frame: "A few things I'd like to run past you that we have in progress looking ahead to next season" or equivalent. Names the lookahead context.
3. **Numbered list**, one item per topic. Each item is its own paragraph (or a paragraph + a short bullet list + a follow-up sentence). The number IS the topic separator. No bold section headers.
4. Order items by **what most needs the recipient's engagement**, not chronologically or by your own mental order. In the Nick G email, SME (needed input) came before Patrol Map (FYI with attached email) even though my draft had Patrol Map second.
5. Close with a direct ask line ("Really keen to get your thoughts on these") + a channel offer ("More than happy to chat through any of this on the phone or catch up in person"). Drop the "I just didn't want to keep waiting" self-explanation — the opener already covered the channel choice.

*Connector phrases that link items (instead of headers):*
- "In terms of getting input on these, ..." — links item 2 to item 1
- "On <topic>, ..." — works when there is no prior-item link
- "After our chat <when> about <topic>, ..." — naturally cues the subject in sentence 1
- Avoid "On the SME side" if there is a more contextual link available; prefer the connector that ties items together.

*What to STRIP when the recipient knows the world:*
- Strategic-logic explanations. v5 had "rep support tends to equal leadership support, which keeps the SME work pointed in the same direction as our broader strategy." Nick cut that entirely. He knows. Don't explain it to him.
- Per-recipient spell-outs. v5 had "In practice that means you'd be the channel for SDO, SAC and ARO reps across three of the four groups (Incident Categorisation and Flow, Call Out Systems and Capabilities, AI within IM)." Nick cut that. He trusts Nick G to map it himself.
- Background that the recipient already has from prior conversation. The framing of an idea Nick has discussed with the recipient before is "I had a chat to Alex about X", not "Alex and I have been discussing X" with a paragraph of context.

*Timing and dates:*
- Specific dates ("1 July", "next week or two", "by Friday 12 June") are correct for the convergence email to multiple people who need to coordinate.
- For the single-recipient nudge email, fold timing into the substantive ask as a one-line context: "I'd be keen to hear your thoughts on that approach as we'd probably need to start getting these together soon to have the process moving for next season." No standalone "On timing" paragraph.
- "Soon" + "for next season" can beat "in the next week or two" + "by 1 July" when the recipient is a peer who knows the season rhythm.

*Conversational signals that are correct for this register:*
- "you guys" addressing a small named group
- "etc etc" trailing a list of acronyms
- "had a chat to <name>" rather than "discussed with <name>"
- "We've landed on" rather than "We propose" or "We've decided"
- "I'd be keen to hear" / "Keen to hear" / "Really keen"

*Attachment pointers:*
- Reference the attachment in-line where its detail lives. Nick added "I've attached the email I sent through re that which has some of my thinking" inside the Patrol Map item, pointing the recipient at where the substance lives without re-narrating it.

*Bullets:*
- Use typographic `•` not markdown `-` when the email is going to Outlook. Markdown bullets render as plain hyphens, which read as a typo in a stakeholder email. Either let Outlook's list formatter handle it, or use `•` if writing the raw text for paste.

*Anti-patterns Nick removed from v5:*
- Bold section headers in body
- Multiple short separate paragraphs covering the same item (his SME item is one bullet list + one summarising paragraph; v5 split it into three paragraphs)
- Justifying the channel choice in the close (already done in the opener)
- "Three things I'd value your input on" memo-agenda phrasing
- "kicking around" when "had a chat" is more concrete

*What I should NOT carry over from this memory:*
- This is the **known-recipient multi-topic nudge** form. For a convergence email to multiple stakeholders with by-Friday asks per person (Email 2 to Nick G + Alex + Dave), the table-and-named-asks structure is correct. Don't collapse those into numbered chat-points.
- For very formal external comms (Board, exec audience Nick doesn't know personally, regulator), the headed-prose structure with pyramid framing is still correct. The "chat" shape is specifically for peer-to-peer.
