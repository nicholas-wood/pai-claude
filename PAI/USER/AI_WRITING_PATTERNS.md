# AI Writing Patterns — Writing As or For Nick

> Distinct from WRITINGSTYLE.md (which describes how Nick writes himself).
> This file tells the DA how to write in his voice or on his behalf.
> Last updated: 2026-06-09 (post Heidi cold-outreach session).

---

## The core distinction

Nick's writing has two modes. The DA must identify which is in play before generating a word.

**Organisational / team mode** (annual reports, board papers, strategy documents, role applications):
The author is collective. "We" is the default. Nick is a contributor and guide, not the hero. Achievements belong to the team. Nick's name appears only where a question explicitly asks for his individual role.

**Positioning / first-person mode** (cover letters, interview answers, LinkedIn, exec bios):
The author is Nick, first person singular, with a deliberate story arc. Allusion over enumeration. The reader has already seen the list; give them meaning, not repetition.

Applying the wrong mode is a serious error. A cover letter written in team voice sounds evasive. A team report written in "I" voice sounds arrogant.

## P0 — Credibility killers (always scrub)

These phrases appear in generated text and actively annoy Nick. Remove before delivery, no exceptions.

- "Let me know if..." closers
- "I hope this helps" / "Hope that helps"
- "I'd be happy to..."
- "Feel free to..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "In conclusion..." / "To summarise..."
- "As an AI language model..."
- "Certainly!" / "Of course!" / "Absolutely!"
- Any opener that praises the request before responding to it

## P1 — AI smell (almost always remove)

- "Here's the thing..."
- "Here's how it works..."
- "Not just X — Y" (em dash construction; also violates the no-em-dash rule)
- "The cool part?"
- "Let's dive in"
- "Game-changer", "transformative", "unprecedented" applied to Nick's own work
- "This is a great opportunity to..."

## P2 — Voice rules when writing AS Nick

### Use his actual verbs

Nick does not "facilitate" things; he runs them. He does not "leverage synergies"; he "builds on" something. The pattern from his prose:

- "delivered" not "facilitated delivery of"
- "refreshed" not "undertook a refresh of"
- "built" not "developed a capability for"
- "brought [X] into [system]" not "integrated [X] into the existing [system] framework"

### Apply the purpose clause

Every item in a list needs a purpose clause that names what it achieves, not just what it is. Do not leave the reader to infer the point.

Wrong: "Drawer dishwasher in the members' kitchenette."
Right: "Drawer dishwasher in the members' kitchenette. Reduces friction for patrolling members who are already donating their time to the club." (from Board Proposal)

### State the number when Nick has it

If a real figure exists, use it. Do not soften to "several", "many", or "significant". Nick uses "674 incidents", "241 major first aids", "under six months", "over 50 hours per week". When you do not have the figure, say "TBC" or note the assumption explicitly.

### Separation of reasoning and conclusion

Nick does not pack both the reasoning and the conclusion into one paragraph. Write the conclusion first (pyramid principle), then the supporting reasoning. If there are multiple independent reasons, number them.

### The close

Never pad the ending. Do not add "I look forward to hearing from you" unless Nick explicitly requests a formal closer. The last sentence should carry weight on its own. Stop there.

## P3 — Voice rules when writing FOR Nick (on his behalf)

These rules apply when drafting material Nick will review and send, but which starts from the DA.

### Never invent facts or outcomes

Nick's career notes include explicit guidance: do not invent. If a figure is missing, use a placeholder. If an outcome is uncertain, frame it as an assumption. Invented specifics will be caught and corrected in review, and they damage trust in the draft.

### Allusion before enumeration in narrative pieces

Do not open a cover letter or an interview answer with a list of achievements. Open with a moment, a turning point, or a claim. Let the specifics emerge as evidence for the claim. The evidence Nick has given about his own preference:

> "I want to share my journey, not list what they can read." (BCG prep feedback, 2026-05-28)

The pattern he approved for his own CV walkthrough: one concrete scene (the Terrigal workshop) that acts as a "Trojan horse" for the full story.

### The "no traditional background" stance

Nick does not apologise for his background or frame himself as non-traditional. Do not use phrases like "while I do not have direct experience in X" or "although my background is in engineering rather than consulting". Per his positioning notes: "There is no traditional consulting background." Lead with what he has built and what it demonstrates.

### Collective credit in operational and governance writing

In reports, role applications, and governance documents:

- Frame achievements as team achievements: "we delivered", "the team built", "we worked together to..."
- Reserve "I" for: direct questions about his personal role, his specific contribution, or first-person narrative contexts.
- Do not use comparative superlatives ("most significant season we have seen", "best outcome in the organisation's history"). State the work plainly.
- Close with a forward, contributing note. Not "I am proud of my achievements" but "I am proud of what the team achieved, and I want to stay involved in seeing this work continue." (Reapplication)

### Cover letter structure

Nick's approved pattern, stated explicitly in his positioning notes:

> "What sets me apart -> how I built it -> why this role."

No filler openers. No apology paragraphs. No comparisons to other candidates. Pyramid-principle structured. End cleanly.

## P4 — Mode-specific conventions

### Board papers and planning documents

- Motion or recommendation at the top, before the rationale.
- Explicit scope and explicit exclusions, with reasons for each.
- "This paper is [not] a commitment to..." where the board might otherwise read it as one.
- Tables for item catalogues, with ID column for stable reference.
- "Open questions" section for anything genuinely unresolved.

### Operational reports (annual reports, role reapplications)

- Group achievements by domain or system, not by chronology.
- Each bullet names what was done and why it matters.
- Results section uses real numbers: incidents, forms, members, clubs.
- Thank-you section is brief, named, and specific. No generic "to all stakeholders".

### Resume and career positioning

- Profile paragraph: differentiator first, proof second, ambition third.
- Each role entry: one framing sentence, then bullet points. Bullets lead with a verb and include a quantified outcome where available.
- No "responsible for" or "involved in". Every bullet is "did X, which produced Y".
- Skills section is a flat list of technologies and methods. No self-assessment levels.

### Cold outreach (gatekeeper or connector)

Distinct from relational outreach (warm ex-colleague register, WRITINGSTYLE.md §12) and from gatekeeper-only cold register (WRITINGSTYLE.md §13). Cold outreach to people Nick does not know but who could move his application forward has two flavours; both share the structural spine, the register and ask differ.

**Gatekeeper** — recruiter, TA partner, hiring manager. They are doing their job; they want role + name + angle in five seconds. Register is plain (see WRITINGSTYLE.md §13). Subject line is transactional and scannable: `<Role> application: Nicholas Wood`. Close folds CV attached + redirect-if-not-you + what's wanted next into a single triage-friendly paragraph.

**Connector** — someone on the team who can bridge to a gatekeeper. Often surfaced via a public LinkedIn post about company growth or hiring. Register is slightly warmer than gatekeeper; a post-acknowledgment opener is appropriate. Subject (InMail) is post-anchored, not role-anchored: `Loved your <Company> growth post, keen to connect with the team`. The ask is small — a name or a direction, not a meeting — so the recipient can respond in thirty seconds without commitment: `Long shot, but if you happen to know the hiring manager or the TA partner...`.

**Shared structural pattern (both flavours):**

1. Opener — state role and application date; acknowledge the direct reach-out without apologising for it. Connector variant references their post here.
2. JD-pattern observation — name something specific noticed reading the company's roles. Earns the right to pitch a lane fit; signals homework; avoids projecting hiring intent on them.
3. Lane claim with honest gap — `albeit a bit earlier on` (more polished register) or `just earlier on` (more conversational register).
4. Plain-prose substance paragraph — what Nick has done across the lane, in plain language. No literary metaphors (see WRITINGSTYLE.md §13).
5. Single ask in a triage-friendly close — gatekeeper gets CV + redirect + next-step; connector gets a small-ask request for a name.

**Reusable phrasing (approved, Heidi PM Intelligence outreach, June 2026):**

JD-pattern hook:
> "Reading a few of [Company]'s roles, both [role A] and [role B], what stood out is that you're actively after [profile]. That's exactly the lane I'm in, albeit a bit earlier on, and I think my combo of skills and experience would be a strong fit."

Connector close (small ask):
> "Long shot, but I was wondering if you happen to know the hiring manager or the TA partner you might be able to put me in touch with?"

Gatekeeper close (triage-friendly):
> "CV attached. If you're not the person owning this role, I'd really appreciate it if you could put me in touch with whoever is. Otherwise, would love to connect to discuss more."

**Constraints (failure modes evidenced June 2026):**

- One emotive intensifier per message, at most. Four `really`s in 200 words is the established failure threshold.
- Single connect/intro ask. Duplicating the ask across paragraphs reads as needy and cannibalises the structural pivot.
- No literary metaphors in gatekeeper register (seam, through-line, surface, receipts — all rejected in the Thamasi draft review).
- No forced clever phrasings (`inside of, not adjacent to` — rejected in the Nell DM review).
- Subject lines never lead with `Application for...` for connector outreach — too transactional for a non-recruiter. Reserve transactional subjects for gatekeeper emails only.
- `would be a strong fit` beats `could be a really great fit` — the latter pairs a hedge ("could") with an intensifier ("really great") in a way that reads anxious. The former is honest and confident.
