# Writing Style — Nick Wood

> Evidence-grounded. Every pattern below is backed by a real quote from his prose.
> Last updated: 2026-06-09 (PM, post Heidi cold-outreach session). Source documents listed at the end.

---

## Voice

Nick writes as a capable, direct practitioner who has earned the right to say what he thinks. The prose is clean and forward-moving: it does not hedge, it does not flatter, and it does not inflate. In operational documents he writes as part of a team ("we delivered", "we built"), keeping himself at one remove from the achievement. In positioning and interview material the voice sharpens into first person and gets more deliberately structured, with a story arc rather than a list. Across all registers the thinking is visible in the shape of the prose: problem stated, action described, consequence named. The register is always professional but never stiff; there is warmth in operational reports and occasional dry precision ("still to launch next season", "idle for four years") that signals a writer who noticed things.

---

## Rules

**Hard rules — no exceptions:**

- No em dashes. Use hyphens, commas, colons, or separate sentences instead.
- Australian English spelling throughout: colour, organisation, recognised, programme, endeavour, etc.
- No sycophancy, no validation loops. No praise before critique.
- No generic advice or consultancy filler. Every sentence does work.
- No over-explaining obvious points. The reader is capable.
- No passive hedging. Own the claim or drop it.
- No comparison to other candidates or self-deprecation about background.
- Recommendation first, then reasoning (pyramid principle applies to all documents).
- No filler openers ("It is a pleasure to...", "I am writing to...").
- No apology paragraphs.
- No em dashes. (Restated deliberately. He is emphatic.)

**Defaults:**

- Collective and humble in role/team documents. "We" as the default; "I" only where a question explicitly asks for his role.
- Avoid comparative superlatives in organisational contexts ("most transformative", "best ever"). State the work plainly.
- Close role applications on a contributing note, not a self-promoting one.
- Distinguish confirmed facts from working assumptions from inferences. Never invent.

## Patterns (evidenced)

### 1. Operational verbs, not nominalisations

Nick names actions rather than dressing them in noun phrases. He writes "we refreshed every callsign across the state" not "we conducted a refresh of callsign terminology." Verbs do the structural work; the noun is the object, not the action itself.

> "We refreshed every callsign across the state so they are clearer and quicker to use." (Annual Report)

> "We brought the medication register into LIMSOC, turning a manual process into a single, reliable digital record." (Annual Report)

### 2. Purpose clause appended to every item

In lists and tables, each item is followed by a short clause that names what it achieves. He does not leave the reader to infer the point. This pattern runs consistently across the board proposal, the master plan, and the annual report.

> "Reduces friction for patrolling members who are already donating their time to the club." (Board Proposal, KIT-01)

> "Removes manual reconciliation between hires and access provisioning, reducing the ongoing workforce burden carried by club volunteers and staff." (Board Proposal, SEC-04)

### 3. "The change in a line" summary before the detail

In complex documents Nick states the transformation in a single concrete sentence before the explanation follows. He does this once, precisely, then moves into the evidence.

> "The change in a line: from 'wow, there is crap everywhere' to 'wow, this is an impressive clubhouse.'" (Board Proposal, project framing)

> "Core platform message: 'Right information, right people, right time.'" (LIMSOC facts file)

### 4. Numbered drivers when the case has multiple independent inputs

When justifying a recommendation that has more than one reason, Nick enumerates them cleanly and names them. He does not present compound reasoning in a single paragraph; he separates and labels.

> "Four drivers point to acting in the current cycle rather than continuing to defer." [followed by four numbered items] (Board Proposal, §3)

> "Three things make now the moment." [followed by three reasons] (BCG prep, Why Now draft)

### 5. Allusion over enumeration in narrative contexts

In interview material and positioning, Nick deliberately avoids reciting lists. He references experiences by what they meant, not by naming them. A single scene carries the weight.

> "A few months ago I sat in a workshop in Terrigal where a room of senior leaders were tangled up confusing applications with data." (BCG CV walkthrough draft)

> "Surf Life Saving became my laboratory for everything else." (career positioning notes)

This is an explicit preference stated in his own words: "I want to share my journey, not list what they can read."

### 6. Direct acknowledgment of constraint or error, without softening

When something did not work or a constraint applies, Nick states it plainly. He does not bury the admission or frame it abstractly.

> "Changing something as ingrained as callsigns asks members to relearn a habit, and there were some slight refinements as we moved through the season." (Reapplication, Q4)

> "The problem was the delivery. I underweighted how the message would land for people who had been working inside a system they hadn't built." (BCG Story Bank, Failure story)

### 7. Short declarative close that stops cleanly

Documents end without trailing summary sentences or a restatement of everything that came before. The last sentence earns its own weight and then stops.

> "There is much more to come." (Annual Report, close of results paragraph)

> "Strong opinions, loosely held. I want to be proven wrong." (BCG CV walkthrough, final lines)

> "End of paper." (Board Proposal)

### 8. Concrete numbers as grounding, not inflation

Nick uses specific quantities to anchor a claim. He does not round up or approximate when a real number is available.

> "This season 674 incidents were managed through LIMSOC and 1,330 first aid forms were recorded, including 241 major first aids." (Annual Report)

> "Reduced manual effort by over 50 hours per week." (Resume, Macquarie)

> "At current functions performance, the term deposit balance is fully restored in under six months." (Board Proposal, §5.2)

### 9. Explicit scope and exclusion in planning documents

Nick names what is in scope and what is not, and gives the reasoning for exclusions. This prevents scope creep and signals disciplined thinking.

> "Items deliberately excluded from bundle: PA work, letterbox, wetsuit racks, washing machine... small, independent, can be done by club volunteers or single trades on demand without coordination overhead." (Master Plan, §3)

> "This paper is not a funding commitment paper. Final dollar figures are not yet available, because quotes have not yet been obtained." (Board Proposal, §3)

### 10. Parallelism across list items, break for the outlier

Within bullet lists, items share a grammatical structure. When one item is genuinely different in nature (a caveat, a forward note), it appears as a standalone sentence outside the list rather than inside it.

> "PA-02 (testing process) excluded — no cost." (Master Plan, end of §4)

### 11. Identity surfaces lead with drive, not credentials

For self-positioning surfaces (LinkedIn headline, bio, short profile copy) Nick writes what drives him, not what he has done. He explicitly rejects achievement-listing in identity copy: no company names, no project names, no title chips. The framing names motivations and the shape of his interest. Hybrid identities use the `×` multiplication operator rather than `+` to signal that the combination is the differentiator, not a sum.

> "Builder × Strategist | Aligning visions, building value, driven by impact | Hard problems, real ownership, AI as multiplier" (LinkedIn headline, June 2026)

Stated preference in his own words:

> "I really want to capture my drive and motivations, my ambitions and my passions rather than listing titles/positions."

Concrete proof is acceptable when honest (e.g. "GenAI delivery at ANZ Plus"); abstract slogan claims are not ("bridge between business and tech", "turning complex problems into scalable solutions" — both rejected as too generic, too consultant-coded).

### 12. Warm ex-colleague register in relational outreach

When reaching out to former colleagues — referral asks, catch-ups, re-connects — the register is warm-but-not-claimed-friendship. The structure is recognisable: brief congratulations on their news, explicit acknowledgement of the time gap, honest one-paragraph update on his own trajectory, the ask woven into prose rather than bulleted, and a no-pressure tail. Australian casual idioms appear naturally ("Been a bit", "down Melb way", "looked really interesting"). Parentheticals carry personal sentiment without disrupting the main message. Question marks soften asks. Exclamation marks signal warmth at the close.

In this register specifically, the punctuation set tightens: **no dashes of any kind (em, en, hyphen) and no colons**. Periods, commas, exclamation marks, question marks, parentheses, apostrophes only.

> "Been a bit since the ANZ Plus days. I finished up at ANZ in April. I've been doing some project and independent work, and also being quite intentional before looking for new roles. I'm looking now though, and the virgin AI role looked really interesting. It fits really well with what I've been doing since with my own work, and where I want to head, building on what we did at ANZ. (Feels like that was so long ago now with how quickly it's all moved!)" (Outreach to ex-colleague at Virgin Australia, June 2026)

> "Would love a referral if you would be open to it?" (Soft ask via question mark, same message)

> "Would be great to catch up if you're in Melb soon or lets do a call!" (Warm close with exclamation, casual idiom)

Stated preference in his own words:

> "There shouldn't be dashes of any type, or colons in the message... a bit more of a relaxed tone."

This register applies to relational/personal messages only. Formal documents, board papers, applications, and operational reports remain governed by the existing patterns and the universal hard rules.

### 13. Functional register for recruiter / TA cold outreach

When the recipient is a recruiter, talent acquisition partner, or hiring manager being approached cold, the register is plainer than any other writing surface Nick uses. Literary moves — metaphors, abstract nouns ("the seam", "the through-line", "the surface"), idiomatic colour ("the CV has the receipts") — read as a candidate trying too hard. The recipient is triaging at speed; literary register signals someone who would rather sound clever than be useful.

The structural pattern that works:

- Plain prose. State what was applied for and when. Acknowledge the direct reach-out without apologising for it.
- Earn the right to pitch by naming a specific observation about their roles, not yours.
- Claim the lane confidently with the gap named honestly: `albeit a bit earlier on` or `just earlier on`.
- A single substantive paragraph in plain language about what's been done across the lane.
- Triage-friendly close: CV attached, redirect-if-not-you, what's actually wanted next — all in one paragraph.

Evidenced contrast (Thamasi Perera email, Heidi PM Intelligence, June 2026):

Rejected draft:
> "I'm a senior engineer who has spent most of the last six years working at the seam between engineering, product and strategy. The pattern across that work has been the same: take an ambiguous problem, build something real, ship it, then make the case for what gets built next. The CV gives you the receipts."

Approved replacement:
> "Over the last six years I've worked across engineering, product and strategy, mostly at the point where AI or new technical capability has to become a product real users can trust."

Stated preference in his own words:

> "CV has the receipts, really don't like that." (Thamasi draft review, 2026-06-09)

> "Trying too hard. Functional emails to recruiters should be plainer than cover letters and exec audiences." (recap acknowledgment, same session)

This register applies to recruiter / TA / hiring manager cold outreach only. Cover letters, exec audiences, board papers, and interview narrative retain literary structure where it earns its keep — §5 (allusion over enumeration) and §11 (drive over credentials) still govern there. Connector cold outreach (a non-recruiter team member who can bridge to a gatekeeper, e.g. via a public post about company growth) sits between this and §12: slightly warmer than gatekeeper cold outreach, slightly more deliberate than relational warm outreach.

## Anti-patterns (what he never does)

- Em dashes as clause separators.
- Opening sentences that compliment the reader or the opportunity.
- Lists of accomplishments without explanation of impact.
- Passive constructions where an agent can be named.
- "Transformative", "game-changing", "unprecedented", "best-in-class" applied to his own work in formal documents.
- Long paragraphs that pack reasoning and conclusion into the same block without separation.
- Closing with "please do not hesitate to contact me" or equivalent.
- Restating the structure of a document before delivering it ("In section one I will cover...").
- AI-ism phrases: "it's worth noting", "it's important to note", "let me know if", "happy to help", "to summarise".
- Self-deprecation about background or experience level.
- Comparison to other candidates or other organisations.
- Abstract slogan claims in identity copy ("bridge between business and tech", "thoughtful AI mandate", "turning complex problems into scalable solutions"). The claim must be either concrete proof or genuine identity, never a generic phrase that could belong to anyone.
- Forced-sounding update intros in relational messages ("Quick update from my side", "I'm thrilled to announce", "I am excited to share"). The opening flows from the prior context; it does not announce itself.
- Listing companies, project names, or job titles in LinkedIn headlines, bios, or other identity surfaces.
- Mixing `+` and `×` for hybrid framings. `×` is the convention.
