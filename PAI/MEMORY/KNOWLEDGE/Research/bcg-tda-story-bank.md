# BCG TDA Behavioural Story Bank (STAR+R)

## Summary

Full set of drafted STAR+R behavioural stories (Situation, Task, Action, Result, Reflection) for Nick's BCG TDA interview process. Target length: ~90 seconds spoken (~230-280 words per story). These stories constitute the retrieval-ready proof points for Nick's career narrative, covering leadership, failure, conflict, persuasion, technical translation, ethical judgment, and technical decision-making. Highest priority for TDA: Story M (tech translation at SLSA Terrigal board workshop).

## Key Facts

**Story I -- Leadership through ambiguity (Asset Master at Macquarie):**
- Regulatory deadline, business modernisation goal, and engineering anxiety all in conflict
- Resolved by separating constraints (regulatory, hard dates) from preferences (business features, no date) from anxieties (delivery risk)
- Staged architecture: regulatory-compliant first wave with extension points for later capabilities
- Result: hit the regulatory deadline; subsequent capabilities built on the same foundation
- Reflection: ambiguity resolves fastest when you separate constraints from preferences

**Story J -- Failure (Early Macquarie feedback):**
- Joined an underperforming platform team; surfaced performance gaps directly with data
- The substance was right; the delivery was wrong -- didn't do the work to understand how it would land for people inside the system
- Some relationships took months to repair; platform issues addressed more slowly than needed
- Reflection: being right on the substance is necessary but not sufficient; now does explicit pre-work on how a message will land for each person in the room

**Story K (primary) -- Conflict with a peer (LSV callsigns with Dave):**
- Data-driven simplification of operational callsigns; some removed units turned out to have operational value not in the data
- Rather than defending the first decision, acknowledged being wrong on the record; brought back some callsigns
- Result: cleaner operational structure; relationship with Dave now allows direct challenge built on trust
- Reflection: being visibly wrong -- on the record -- is what made the relationship work

**Story K (alternative) -- Conflict with a peer (Zak at ANZ, generative AI pace):**
- Disagreed on pace of generative AI adoption in a regulated banking context; Nick wanted faster, Zak wanted more cautious
- Took it offline; worked through underlying interests (Nick: capability and learning velocity; Zak: integration quality and risk posture in a regulated context)
- Agreed on a pace between the two positions with explicit guardrails
- Reflection: separating positions from interests -- they weren't disagreeing about pace, but about what each was accountable for

**Story L -- Persuading a senior stakeholder (SLSA Product Manager hire):**
- Advocated for SLSA hiring its first ever product manager; initially laughed at
- Changed approach: stopped selling the role title; started selling the absence of one and what it was costing (decisions made without rigour, digital spend going to whoever asked first)
- Socialised individually with board members; gave executive room to land the decision themselves
- Result: SLSA hired its first PM; role has expanded; current strategic plan calls for growing the product practice
- Reflection: influencing without authority requires people to feel they reached the conclusion, not that they were dragged to it

**Story M -- Tech translation to executive (SLSA Terrigal board workshop) [TDA-critical]:**
- Joint SLSA Board and Executive Management Group workshop; facilitator and room confused applications with data -- conversation looping
- Asked on a moment's notice to step up and clear it up
- Drew three boxes at the whiteboard: applications (what people use), data (long-lived asset underneath), platforms (foundation both run on); used examples the room knew (membership app, incident system, volunteer register)
- Result: room shifted; rest of the workshop ran on the right abstraction; board adopted structures already being put in place with the new PM
- Reflection: the highest-leverage role for someone with technical depth in an executive room is not to add detail -- it is to take confusing technical reality and make it boring

**Story N -- Ethical judgment (LIMSOC bid quote revision):**
- Inherited a bid where the original quote (prepared before Nick's involvement) understated the work, favouring the firm short-term
- Revised the quote to the higher, correct number; made the change visible internally
- Reflection: a visible correction sets the tone for how the engagement runs; a quiet correction only protects you

**Story O -- Delivering through resistance (SLSA product structures post-hire):**
- After SLSA hired the PM, organisational antibodies persisted; same people who resisted the hire resisted the new structures
- Three levers: picked battles deliberately, made structures visible (attributed good and bad decisions to presence/absence of process), used board-level moments to legitimise structures
- Reflection: persistent resistance breaks by visible outcomes accumulated over time in front of people who can legitimise the new way

**Story P -- High-stakes technical decision (Asset Master microservices vs monolith):**
- Technical lead wanted monolith; Nick believed microservices was correct (aligned with business objectives for independent extension; supported by industry data and comparable platforms)
- Architecture team came on board; proposal carried; platform extended in ways monolith would have made painful
- Reflection: proudest technical call in substance; least proud in process -- winning the argument pushed the tech lead away; the lesson is that getting the right answer and bringing the senior people on the build with you are not optional alternatives
